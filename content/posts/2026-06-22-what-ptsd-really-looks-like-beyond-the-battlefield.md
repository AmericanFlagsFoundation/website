June is PTSD Awareness Month — a time set aside to push back against the myths, correct the record, and remind anyone carrying a weight they didn't ask for: what you're experiencing has a name, it has science behind it, and it has real, effective treatment.

Post-traumatic stress disorder is one of the most misunderstood conditions in mental health. The word "trauma" conjures images of combat zones and catastrophic accidents. The word "disorder" implies something broken beyond repair. Neither picture is accurate, and together they have kept millions of people from recognizing themselves in the diagnosis — and getting help.

## The Numbers Are Larger Than Most People Realize

- Approximately **13 million Americans** are living with PTSD in any given year, according to the National Center for PTSD.
- **About 70 percent of adults** in the U.S. will experience at least one traumatic event in their lifetime. Of those, roughly 20 percent will develop PTSD.
- PTSD affects **women at roughly twice the rate of men** — not because women are less resilient, but because the types of trauma most linked to PTSD (sexual violence, childhood abuse, domestic violence) disproportionately affect women.
- Among **veterans**, roughly 11 to 20 percent of those who served in Operation Iraqi Freedom or Enduring Freedom experience PTSD in a given year.
- PTSD is consistently **underdiagnosed** — research suggests that many people live with symptoms for years before receiving an accurate diagnosis or any treatment.

These numbers are not meant to alarm. They're meant to normalize. PTSD is not rare. It is not a sign of weakness. And it is not permanent.

## What PTSD Actually Looks Like

PTSD does not always look like flinching at loud noises or having vivid combat flashbacks. More often, it looks like:

- **Avoiding** places, people, or conversations that trigger even a vague sense of unease — without always knowing why
- **Hypervigilance** — a constant low-grade scanning of the environment for threat, exhausting to maintain and hard to turn off
- **Sleep disruption** — nightmares, difficulty falling asleep, waking in the night with the heart racing
- **Emotional numbing** — feeling detached from people you love, unable to feel joy or connection
- **Intrusive memories** — not necessarily cinematic flashbacks, but fragments: a smell, a sound, a moment that surfaces unbidden and pulls attention backward
- **Negative cognitions** — beliefs like *I should have done something*, *I can't trust anyone*, or *The world is fundamentally unsafe* that feel like facts rather than symptoms

Recognizing these patterns in yourself — or in someone you love — is the first step toward something better.

## The Science Is Genuinely Hopeful

Here is what the research is clear on: **PTSD is one of the most treatable mental health conditions we know of.**

Two therapies in particular have decades of evidence behind them:

**Prolonged Exposure (PE)** — A structured therapy that helps the brain learn, through repeated safe engagement with traumatic memories and avoided situations, that the threat has passed. Study after study shows significant symptom reduction, often within 8 to 15 sessions.

**EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)** — A therapy that uses bilateral stimulation (typically guided eye movement) to help the brain process traumatic memories in a way that reduces their emotional charge. The World Health Organization recommends EMDR for PTSD in both adults and children.

**Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)** is another first-line treatment, specifically targeting the distorted beliefs trauma tends to leave behind.

Medication — particularly SSRIs like sertraline and paroxetine — can also be a meaningful part of treatment, especially in combination with therapy.

The point: this is not a condition people are meant to white-knuckle through alone. Treatment works. People recover. Not back to who they were before — but forward to someone who carries the past without being ruled by it.

## The Stigma Is Still the Barrier

Despite everything science has learned, stigma remains the single most significant obstacle between people with PTSD and effective care. Particularly among men. Particularly in cultures — military, first responder, sports — where self-reliance is a core identity.

The irony is that seeking help *is* the harder, more demanding thing. Sitting with a therapist and revisiting painful memories in a structured, intentional way takes more courage than silence. The people who pursue treatment are not the ones who couldn't handle it. They're the ones who decided they deserved better.

If you've been carrying something heavy and haven't told anyone — June is as good a month as any to start.

## Resources

**988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** — Call or text **988** (free, 24/7, confidential). Veterans can press 1 for specialized support.

**Crisis Text Line** — Text HOME to **741741** to connect with a trained crisis counselor via text.

**National Center for PTSD** — [ptsd.va.gov](https://www.ptsd.va.gov) — Evidence-based information, self-assessment tools, and a therapist finder.

**SAMHSA National Helpline** — 1-800-662-4357 — Free, confidential treatment referrals and information, 24/7.

*You don't have to be in crisis to reach out. You just have to decide you're worth the call.*
