There's a phrase Renee Williams heard so many times she started to believe it.

*Too far gone.*

She heard it from a family member who'd run out of patience. She heard it — not in words, but in body language — from the emergency room staff who recognized her face for the third time in eighteen months. She heard it, most devastatingly, in her own head, at two in the morning, when sobriety felt like a foreign country she'd never have a passport to.

"I had a list in my mind of all the reasons I was beyond help," Renee says now, sitting in the community center she runs in Knoxville, Tennessee. "The years I'd lost. The people I'd hurt. The chances I'd burned. I had that list memorized."

What Renee didn't have, back then, was anyone willing to argue with the list.

Until a woman named Phyllis did.

---

## A Caseworker Who Refused to Move On

Phyllis Drummond had been a community outreach worker for eleven years when she first met Renee — then 34, in the parking lot of a laundromat where Renee had been sleeping in her car.

"She didn't want to talk to me," Phyllis remembers. "She told me she'd heard every program's pitch and none of it stuck. She was done trying."

Phyllis handed her a sandwich, wrote her number on a paper bag, and left.

She came back three days later. Then again the following week.

"I didn't have a plan," Phyllis admits. "I just knew that if I kept showing up, eventually she'd know someone gave a damn. That's not a clinical strategy. That's just — that's what you do for a person."

This kind of sustained, relational outreach doesn't appear in most public health statistics. It's hard to measure and harder to fund. But community mental health workers and peer support specialists across the country practice some version of it every day: the philosophy that **connection itself is an intervention** — that before someone can accept help, they need to believe they're worth helping.

It took Renee four months to call the number on the paper bag. Phyllis picked up on the first ring.

## What "Community" Actually Means in a Crisis

The word *community* can feel abstract — a warm concept that dissolves the moment you actually need something from it.

But for people navigating addiction, mental illness, or crisis, community is often the difference between surviving and not. Research consistently shows that social isolation is one of the strongest predictors of both addiction relapse and suicide risk. Conversely, people with even one reliable supportive relationship are significantly more likely to sustain recovery.

In Renee's case, that community was pieced together slowly, imperfectly, over the course of years:

- A **faith-based recovery group** that let her show up in whatever state she was in, without requiring she be "ready" first
- A **transitional housing program** that provided not just a room but structured community meals and weekly check-ins
- A **peer support specialist** — herself in long-term recovery — who met Renee every Thursday at a diner to talk through whatever was hard that week
- Eventually, a **part-time job** at the very community center she now leads

None of these things fixed her. Renee is clear about that. "You can't be fixed by a program," she says. "But you can be held by people long enough to figure out how to hold yourself."

---

## The Other Side of the List

Renee is 41 now. She has seven years of sobriety. She has a daughter she's rebuilding a relationship with, slowly, carefully, with a therapist's guidance.

She also has a laminated card on her desk — a new list. On it are the names of twelve people she personally helped connect to services or support in the last year alone. Women who were, by some measure, *too far gone.* Women who called her at midnight, who showed up at the center in crisis, who said they'd tried everything.

She reads them the old list — the one in her head from those dark years — not to discourage them, but to show them she knows it by heart.

"And then I tell them: that list is a liar," she says. "Because I'm still here. And you're still here. And that means there's still something to work with."

## Resources That Can Help Right Now

If you or someone you love is struggling with mental health, addiction, or crisis — please know that support exists, and that connection is just one call or text away.

**Immediate support:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** — Call or text **988** (24/7, free, confidential)
- **Crisis Text Line** — Text **HELLO** to **741741** (24/7, free, confidential)

**Additional resources:**
- **SAMHSA National Helpline** — 1-800-662-4357 (mental health & addiction, 24/7, free)
- **NAMI Helpline** — 1-800-950-6264 | [nami.org](https://nami.org)

You don't have to earn your way back. You just have to show up.

And if you know someone who might be struggling — consider being a Phyllis. You don't need a degree or a script. You need a phone number written on a paper bag, and the willingness to come back three days later.

That's what community looks like. And it saves lives.
