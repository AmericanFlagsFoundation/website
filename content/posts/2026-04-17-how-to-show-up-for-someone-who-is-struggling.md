Most of us have been there: someone we love is clearly not okay, and we have no idea what to do. We want to help. We desperately don't want to say the wrong thing. So sometimes — without meaning to — we say nothing at all, or we reach for words that land harder than we intended.

Knowing how to support someone who is struggling is a skill. And like any skill, it can be learned.

You don't need to be a therapist. You don't need the perfect words. You just need a few real tools — and the willingness to show up.

## Start by Dropping the Need to Fix It

The most common instinct when someone is hurting is to solve it. We offer advice, silver linings, comparisons ("at least..."), and reasons to feel better. It comes from love. But it almost never helps.

When someone is in pain, the first thing they need is to feel *heard* — not redirected, not problem-solved, not cheered up. Trying to fix the feeling before acknowledging it often leaves people feeling more alone, not less.

**Try this instead:** Reflect what you're hearing. "That sounds really hard." "I can see why you're exhausted." "That makes total sense." These phrases do more heavy lifting than you'd think.

## Ask Before You Advise

One of the most underrated things you can do is ask someone what kind of support they actually want.

"Do you want to vent, or do you want me to help think through it?"

That one question changes everything. It signals that you're here for *them*, not for your own need to feel useful. And it gives the person in pain a small measure of control in a moment where they may feel like they have none.

Different people need different things. Some people want to talk it out. Some people need distraction. Some people just want someone to sit with them quietly. Ask.

## Say the Specific Thing

Vague offers — "Let me know if you need anything!" — are almost never taken up. Not because the person doesn't need help, but because when you're struggling, identifying what you need and asking for it is its own exhausting task.

Instead, be specific and concrete:

- "I'm going to drop off dinner Thursday. What do you like?"
- "I'll drive you to your appointment. Tell me when."
- "I'm free Saturday morning — want to take a walk?"

Specific offers are easy to say yes to. They remove the burden of asking. That's the whole point.

## Learn the Warning Signs — and Don't Look Away

Supporting someone isn't just about being emotionally present. It also means paying attention.

Some signs that someone may need more than a friend right now:

- Withdrawing from people and activities they used to enjoy
- Sleeping far too much or barely at all
- Giving away possessions or saying goodbye in unusual ways
- Talking about feeling like a burden, being hopeless, or having no reason to go on
- A sudden calm after a long period of distress (which can signal a decision has been made)

If you notice these signs, name them gently and directly. *"I've noticed you seem really low lately. I'm worried about you. Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?"*

Asking about suicide does **not** plant the idea. Research is clear on this. It opens a door. And sometimes, that door is the one that saves a life.

## Take Care of Yourself Too

You cannot pour from an empty cup — and supporting someone through a mental health crisis is genuinely hard. It's okay to feel helpless, frustrated, scared, or exhausted. Those feelings don't mean you're failing them.

Set limits on what you can give. Talk to someone you trust about what you're carrying. You don't have to be someone's only support system, and you shouldn't try to be.

Encouraging someone to get professional help is one of the most loving things you can do. "Have you thought about talking to someone? I can help you find someone if that feels like too much." That's not giving up on them — it's advocating for them.

## The Simplest Truth

You don't have to do this perfectly. You just have to keep showing up.

A text that says "thinking of you" on a hard day. Sitting quietly in a waiting room. Calling back when they don't answer the first time. The cumulative weight of small, consistent presence is what people remember when they come out the other side.

You can't rescue someone from their pain. But you can make sure they don't face it alone.

That matters more than you know.
