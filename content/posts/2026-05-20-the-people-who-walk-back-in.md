Marcus was 34 years old the first time he called a crisis line.

He'd been functioning — or doing a good enough impression of it — for years. A steady job. A small apartment. A circle of friends who didn't know he hadn't slept more than three hours a night in months, that he'd stopped opening his mail, that some days the weight of simply being alive felt like something he couldn't carry much further.

The call lasted 22 minutes. He doesn't remember exactly what the counselor said. What he remembers is that the voice on the other end didn't flinch. Didn't try to solve him. Just stayed.

"She made it feel like I was a person, not a problem," Marcus says now. "That sounds like a small thing. It wasn't."

Three years later, Marcus is a certified peer support specialist in Austin, Texas. On Tuesday and Thursday evenings, he runs a drop-in group at a community mental health center for men who are, in his words, "trying to figure out if they're okay or not." Most of them aren't sure. Most of them have never told anyone before walking through that door.

He knows what it feels like. That's the whole point.

## What Peer Support Actually Is

Peer support is one of the fastest-growing fields in mental health — and one of the least talked about.

Peer support specialists are people with lived experience of mental illness, addiction, or crisis who are trained to provide support, encouragement, and practical guidance to others facing similar challenges. They are not therapists. They are not case managers. They are people who have been through it, who did the work, and who are now using that experience as a resource.

The research behind peer support is substantial. Studies consistently show that peer-delivered services improve engagement in treatment, reduce hospitalization rates, decrease feelings of isolation, and increase hope. A 2020 review published in *Psychiatric Services* found that peer support interventions were associated with significant improvements in self-reported recovery, empowerment, and quality of life.

But the data only tells part of the story. The other part sounds like Marcus — and like the people he sits across from every Tuesday.

"You can tell someone 'it gets better' and they'll nod," he says. "Or you can tell them 'I was where you are, and here's what I did, and here's where I am now' — and something different happens in the room. Something clicks."

## A Movement Built by the People Who Needed It

Across the country, communities are building mental health infrastructure from the inside out — with people who have lived experience at the center of it.

In rural Tennessee, a network of peer support volunteers reaches people in counties where the nearest therapist is an hour away. In Detroit, a coalition of faith communities has trained more than 200 congregants as mental health first responders, most of them people who have navigated their own crises. In Phoenix, a peer-run respite house offers a voluntary, short-term alternative to psychiatric hospitalization — a place to stabilize, rest, and be seen.

These programs don't replace clinical care. They extend it. They reach into the gaps — the middle-of-the-night gap, the "I don't know if this is serious enough to call a doctor" gap, the "I'm too ashamed to tell anyone I know" gap.

And they are staffed, in large part, by people who fell into those gaps themselves and climbed out.

## What Recovery Makes Possible

One of the things that doesn't get talked about enough in mental health conversations is what happens after the hard part.

Not the absence of hard parts — recovery rarely means the end of struggle. But what becomes possible when someone has moved through something dark and found, on the other side, that they are still standing. That they are, in some ways they couldn't have predicted, more themselves.

Marcus talks about this carefully, the way people talk about things they're still figuring out.

"I spent a long time thinking that what I went through was just... something that happened to me. Something bad. And it was bad. But I don't know how to explain it — it also gave me something. It gave me the ability to sit in that room with someone who is scared and not be scared *for* them. To know that they can make it through. Because I made it through."

He pauses.

"I wouldn't give it back. Isn't that the weirdest thing? I wouldn't give it back."

## You Are Someone's Proof

If you are in a hard season right now, here is something worth holding onto:

The person leading Marcus's group on Tuesday nights wasn't born knowing how to do that. They learned — through suffering, through help, through time, through practice. They walked back in because someone once stayed on the line for them.

Recovery doesn't just change one life. It compounds. It teaches. It makes things possible that weren't possible before.

If you're struggling, reaching out is the first step. And if you've made it through — if you've got your own version of the 2 a.m. call in your past — know that your story is not just yours. It's a resource. It's a light.

The world needs more people who will walk back in.
