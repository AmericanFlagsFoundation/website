Renee can tell you the exact gas station.

Quiktrip on Highway 6. The one with the broken neon sign on the "Q." She knows because she sat in the parking lot for forty-five minutes that night two years ago, phone in her hand, telling herself she wasn't the kind of person who made those kinds of calls.

She was forty-one years old. She had a daughter in middle school. She coached her daughter's soccer team on Saturdays. She was, by every external measure, holding it together.

She was also thinking, quietly and with terrifying clarity, that she was done.

"I had this whole conversation with myself about why calling was pointless," she says. "I told myself I wasn't in 'real' crisis. That the line was for people who were really in trouble, not just someone sitting in a parking lot feeling nothing. I almost convinced myself."

She made the call anyway. She talked to a counselor named DeShawn for forty-two minutes. She went home. And two years later, she is a peer support specialist who has sat with dozens of people in their own parking-lot moments — guiding them through the same door she barely let herself open.

## The Lie That Almost Won

What almost stopped Renee was a story she'd internalized without realizing it: that crisis resources were for other people. For people visibly falling apart. For people whose suffering was legible, dramatic, undeniable. Not for someone who could still go to work. Not for someone who still made dinner most nights.

It's a story a lot of people believe. And it's one of the most dangerous.

"Crisis doesn't always look like what you see in movies," says Dr. Alicia Tomás, a licensed clinical social worker who trains crisis counselors in the Midwest. "Sometimes it looks like someone sitting very still, feeling completely empty, completely alone in a moment when they can't see a way forward. The absence of visible chaos doesn't mean the danger isn't real."

The 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — which launched nationally in 2022 and has since answered tens of millions of calls and texts — was designed precisely for the full spectrum of crisis, not just the visible extreme. Counselors are trained to meet people wherever they are: terrified, numb, uncertain, half-convinced they shouldn't even be on the line.

DeShawn, the counselor Renee spoke with that night, told her something she's repeated dozens of times since: *"You don't have to be sure it's an emergency to call. Uncertainty is exactly when you call."*

## What Recovery Actually Looks Like

Renee didn't walk out of that phone call fixed. That's not how it works, and she's honest about it.

What she had, for the first time in months, was a thread. A next step. DeShawn helped her identify a therapist in her network who had availability that week. He walked her through one breathing exercise, which she describes as "feeling almost embarrassingly simple but actually helping." And he asked her to send a text to one person she trusted before she started her car.

She texted her sister. Her sister called back in four minutes.

Over the next year, Renee went to therapy. She went on medication. She told her daughter, carefully and age-appropriately, that she'd been going through something hard and was getting help — and watched her daughter's face shift from vague worry to visible relief that there were words for it now.

She also, slowly, got angry. Not at herself. At the silence.

"I thought about how many people I knew who were probably sitting in their own parking lots," she says. "People who had convinced themselves they didn't count. I kept thinking about all of us, scattered around, suffering alone, not knowing we all felt the same way."

## From Receiver to Giver

Eighteen months after that phone call, Renee completed her Peer Support Specialist certification. She now works part-time for a community mental health organization, serving on a mobile crisis team that responds alongside clinicians to people in non-violent mental health emergencies in her county.

She also volunteers two nights a week on a peer warm line — the kind of line she'd never heard of before her own crisis. Not a 911-level emergency response. A human voice. A peer who's been there. Someone to talk to before you're in the parking lot, or while you're in it, or after you've gone home and the house is too quiet.

"I try to be the version of DeShawn for somebody else," she says. "Someone who says: you called, you're here, that already took courage, let's figure out what's next."

Her daughter, now fourteen, knows what her mom does. She asked once if it was hard — hearing people in pain all the time.

Renee thought about it.

"I told her it's hard when I can't help. But most of the time I can help. And that's a really good feeling."

## What Renee Wants You to Know

She has a short list. She shares it when she speaks at trainings and church basements and community events:

**You do not have to be "bad enough" to reach out.** There is no threshold you have to hit. If you're struggling, that is enough.

**The call can be short.** You don't have to have all the words. You can say *I don't know why I'm calling, I just needed to call.* That is enough.

**Crisis is not a character flaw.** It is a moment. Moments pass, especially when you're not alone in them.

**Someone has been exactly where you are.** They made it. They are, right now, answering phones and sitting with strangers and hoping you call.

Renee thinks about the parking lot sometimes. The broken "Q." The forty-five minutes. How close she came to driving home without making the call.

She doesn't say she's grateful it happened. That would be too neat. What she says is that she's glad she's still here, that she knows it took more courage than she gave herself credit for, and that she wants other people to know their parking-lot moment doesn't have to be the end of the story.

It can be, if you let it, the place where the next chapter starts.
