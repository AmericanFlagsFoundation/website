title: "The Table Where Men Learn to Ask for Help"
date: "2026-04-29"
excerpt: "Marcus started hosting dinner at his kitchen table for guys in his neighborhood who were quietly struggling. He didn't call it therapy. He called it Tuesday. Two years later, it's become something bigger than he ever imagined."

Marcus will tell you he's not a therapist. He'll say it twice, maybe three times, because he means it.

He's a former Army mechanic, a father of three, and a guy who fixes HVAC systems for a living. What he also is — and what he didn't plan to become — is the person in his neighborhood who opened his kitchen table to men who had nowhere else to take the weight they were carrying.

He calls it Tuesday. Every week, it's just Tuesday.

"I didn't want it to sound like a support group," he says. "Because I knew if I called it that, nobody would come. In my community, in a lot of communities, men don't go to support groups. They go to dinner. So I just made it dinner."

## How Tuesday Started

Three years ago, Marcus was in a place he describes plainly as "nowhere good."

He'd come back from two tours and spent years being the strong one — for his wife, his kids, his crew at work. He'd gotten so good at holding it together on the outside that he stopped noticing what was falling apart on the inside. Then his marriage went through a hard stretch, work slowed down in winter, and one night he sat alone in his garage for four hours not wanting to move.

"I wasn't in crisis the way people imagine crisis," he says. "I wasn't going to do anything. I was just... gone. Empty. And I realized I had nobody to call. Not because nobody cared. Because I'd spent twenty years being the person people called. I didn't know how to be on the other end."

A friend eventually showed up — not because Marcus reached out, but because the friend had noticed. They sat in the garage and talked for two hours about nothing and everything. Marcus drove to his doctor's office the next morning and asked for a referral to a counselor. It took three months to actually go. But he went.

"What changed for me wasn't a hotline. It was a person who showed up," he says. "And then I started thinking — what if I could be that person for somebody else? What if I could make it so men didn't have to wait for someone to notice?"

## Just Dinner

The first Tuesday, Marcus invited four guys he knew were going through hard stretches. Divorce, job loss, a kid in trouble, a grief that wasn't getting lighter. He cooked chili. He told them why they were there: because he'd been in a rough place and someone had shown up for him, and he wanted to pass it forward. He said there were no rules except that nobody had to talk about anything they didn't want to, and nobody was going to tell them to look on the bright side.

One guy cried by the second bowl of chili. Another one didn't say anything significant for three Tuesdays in a row — and then, on the fourth, said something that shifted the whole room.

"Something about sitting down together, sharing food, no agenda — it lowers defenses," Marcus says. "Guys who'd never say 'I'm struggling' will say 'man, I don't know how you're holding it together' and that becomes the same conversation. You just have to come in sideways."

Within six months, word had spread enough that he moved Tuesday to his church's community room to fit twelve people. Then twenty. Now there are three Tuesday groups running in his metro area — the original, one he handed off to a friend across town, and one that started organically in a city two states away when someone who'd moved told people about it.

## What Gets Said on Tuesdays

Marcus is careful about what he shares from inside the room. The trust depends on it. But he talks broadly about patterns he's witnessed.

Men who hadn't said out loud that they were struggling until someone handed them a bowl of food and sat down across from them. Fathers who admitted they were scared of their own moods and didn't know what to do about it. Veterans processing things they'd carried silently for years. Men who came in certain they were fine and left with the name of a therapist someone else had found helpful.

He partners now with a local community mental health center that sends a licensed social worker to connect with anyone who wants a referral after dinner. Not to run the group — it's not clinical, and he's insistent on that — but to be a bridge.

"I'm not trying to fix anybody," he says. "That's not what Tuesday is. Tuesday is just a place where you're allowed to be a person. Where you don't have to be strong the whole time. From there, people figure out what they need."

## The Thing About Showing Up

Marcus's daughter, who is sixteen, helped him set up chairs at the church last fall. She asked him once who all the guys were.

He said they were people who needed a table.

She thought about that. Then she said, "That's a pretty easy thing to give somebody."

He says it stopped him cold. Because she was right, and also wrong. It's easy in the sense that it's simple — food, time, presence, no judgment. It costs almost nothing to provide. But it requires something: a willingness to stop pretending you have everything under control. To admit that showing up, asking for help, sitting down at someone else's table is not weakness.

It is, Marcus says, one of the hardest things he knows how to do. And the most important.

"Every man who comes to Tuesday is doing the hard thing," he says. "They just don't always know it yet. I want them to know it. I want them to know that walking in the door is already courage. Whatever comes next, they already did the hardest part."
